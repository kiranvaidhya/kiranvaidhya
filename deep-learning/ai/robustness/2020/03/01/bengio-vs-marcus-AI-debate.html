The war between connectionism and symbolism has regained traction following some of the rather conspicuous failures of current deep learning system used in practice. Gary Marcus authored the popular science non-fiction book, Rebooting AI to highlight the drawbacks of deep learning systems and to promote the Symbolic AI program. Ranging from the errors seen in self-driving cars to scan cancer diagnosis, deep learning systems have consistently showcased their brittle nature. However, it is important to not forget the significant advances in machine learning triggered by the development of training procedures for neural networks using C/CUDA on GPUs. Hand-crafted features have been replaced by deep neural networks in virtually every domain you can think of. The 2010s has been one of the most successful decades for Artificial Intelligence.

Connectionism is grounded on the idea that there exists an universal learning algorithm which can be trained without hand-crafting a large set of priors. Symbolism, however, says you cannot develop a robust system without encoding a rich knowledge base. This is where Gary Marcus differs from Yoshua Bengio, Yann LeCun and Geoffrey Hinton (the Turing Award winners).

Gary Marcus has been pretty vocal in his idea that deep neural networks trained with on large datasets can be brittle. He has also very rightly called out Open AI for their questionable management driven decisions showcased in GPT-2. Bengio continues to advocate further research into deep learning. He hypothesizes that objective reasoning and awareness can be modeled by neural networks through energy minimization techniques. Yann LeCun and Geoffrey Hinton are bullish about self-supervised learning. It remains to be seen if reasoning can be achieved through neural networks through self/weakly/un-supervised learning methodologies.

Conclusion

Now, I’m not an expert in symbolism but it’s important to isolate concerns regarding marketing and scientific progress. Deep learning is not about replicating human intelligence. It’s a machine learning methodology grounded on elegant computational mathematics. Significant progress has been made and there’s no technical reason for us to shift the methodology. Should we slow down the research and ground the progress on first principles? Yes. Should we regulate AI products in the market and dial down on the claims? Yes. Should we put more resources into symbolic reasoning? I’m not so sure.

Gary Marcus probably has the best interests in terms of scientific research and development but it’s hard to see what exactly he is asking the field to do otherwise. At the time of this writing, Marcus has published some papers about the drawbacks of deep learning systems. I’m not particularly aware of any his peer-reviewed publications on symbolism that’s gained sufficient traction at top conferences or journals. He is the CEO of Robust AI but it needs more than a startup and writing popular science books to drive the field forward in a different direction. It needs collaborations with major institutions and companies. If Gary Marcus really believes in the Symbolic AI program, I would kindly recommend him to fund research projects in collaboration with the MITs, Stanfords and MILAs.
